Levels of Measurement

There are a total of three different levels of measurement.

Nominal Data(Categorical Variable + Discrete)

This data classifies into one group or another.

  • Cannot say that One response is higher/lower than another
    • Basically there is no relationship between the different possibilities
  • Examples: Sex, Race, Insurance Status

Ordinal Data(Discrete)

Collected in ways that has an inherent ranking/ordering, but do not know how much more/less one is -> just that it is more/less

  • Examples: Education level, School Grades, Health Status, finishing position in a race

Interval and Ration Data(Continuous or Discrete)

This is ordered data with a constant scale.

  • Interval data has “no natural zero”
  • Example: Temperature(Interval Data), Birth weight

Natural Zero

  • A true zero measurement value, like height.
  • But year/temperature do not have a natural zero as the choice of a 0 is arbitrary(like freezing point of water or the year 0)

Operationalization

The process in order to turn a vague concept into a precise measurement.

  • Theoretical Construct: The item we are trying to take a measurement of
  • A Measure: refers to the method or tool used to make observations
    • Such as a question in a survey or a behavioral observation
  • Variable: The actual data we utilize within our data sets.

Reliability

Tells you how precisely you are measuring something while validity means how accurate the measure is.

  • Refers to the repeatability or consistency of the measurement.
  • Example: Using a bathroom scale to measure ones weight
    • The scale will return the same weight constantly making it RELIABLE but it could be off by a certain number(inaccurate/invalid).

Test-Retest Reliability

Test the consistency over a time period, will we get the same measurement at a later date.

Inter-Rater Reliability

The consistency across people, so if this measurement is repeated over to a different person is it the same answer.

Parallel forms Reliability

The consistency across theoretically equivalent measurements, across multiple bathroom scales is it the same.

Descriptive statistics is to summaries what we know while inferential statistics is to learn what we do not know from what we know

Samples & Population

Sampling Method

The way/procedure a sample is selected from a population.

Simple Random Sampling

The procedure where every member within the population has the same chance of being selected.

  • Without replacement means removing the sample from the population so they would not repeat.

Most psychology experiments tend to be sampling without replacement because the same person is not allowed to participate in the experiment twice

Stratified Sampling

The procedure where you chose a random sample from every single one of the strata(sub-populations).

  • Example: non-schizophrenic and schizophrenic
    • known as oversampling as a rare group is over-represented through sampling

Snowball Sampling

Basically gain contacts/sampling by asking the initial sample for more people to participate.

  • Useful for a hard to access population

Convenience Sampling

Choose a convenient sample for the research and generally not random

  • Snowball Sampling is one version of this.

Population Parameters

Known as the characteristics of the entire population, such as the population mean and population standard deviation.

Sample Statistics

These are the actual properties from a collected data set such as the sample mean and sample standard deviation.
